[[normalization model of attention (NMA)]]
I really don't understand the claims made in the normalization model of attention. 

What is a suppressive field? Is there suppression within the RF as well? Looks like that. They give an example of suppression when there are two stimuli (one preferred and another non-preferred) in the RF. 

Only in limiting cases do you observe clean contrast and response gain. 

Does suppression depend on how much of the RF the stimulus is covering or is it just a function of stimulus size? This question came about because varying stimulus size will definitely vary the amount of suppression since more of the suppression field is covered. But will having the same stimulus size but varying the receptive field size lead to different suppression? One possibility is that the stimulus drive might be higher if the stimulus is totally within the RF rather than partly within and partly out (for the same stimulus). But this also depends on having identically responding RFs where the response scales with the amount of stimulus in the RF. Or does this response only depend on the contrast of the stimulus?

Higher the stimulus drive, higher the suppressive drive because they are intrinsically related by the suppressive field (so basically some fraction of the stimulus drive). But the surround stimulus will also evoke its own suppressive drive. Can the suppressive field be larger than the receptive field?
